✍ Secondary Essay Prompts (2025-2026)

Prompt 1 (250 words)

Describe an experience where you attempted a task and realized during the process that you were not ready for it. How did this experience impact your approach to trying new things?

Strategy: The lost-MA-job story works perfectly here — she accepted a peds MA job before work auth cleared, realized she literally couldn't legally do it, had to request termination. Real, recent, and shows growth.

Prompt 2 (250 words)

How will your life experiences foster a positive educational environment and benefit your future patients?

Strategy: Canadian-Indian-American identity + Medical Anthropology training + navigating healthcare as a non-citizen. She brings a lived understanding of what it's like to be "other" in the healthcare system.

Prompt 3 (250 words)

Discuss your involvement in a service activity that has impacted your understanding of healthcare and your desire to pursue a career in medicine.

Strategy: Play Atrium at UNC Children's (2.5 years, 100+ hours) or CPALS (2.5 years supporting peds heme-onc). Both are UNC-specific, which strengthens the "coming home" narrative.

Prompt 4 — "Why UNC?" (250 words)

What motivates you to apply to the UNC School of Medicine?

Strategy: You're not discovering UNC — you're coming home. Open with a Marsico Lab memory. Name Dept of Social Medicine + Humanities Scholarly Concentration. Name Kenan Urban Primary Care Scholars (Charlotte). Reference your research. Close with serving NC communities.

Reapplicant Essay (Required)

What has changed about you as a candidate since your last application to medical school? What has made you a stronger applicant?

Framework:

  • • Before: Strong academics + research, but zero paid clinical hours due to visa restrictions
  • • The pivot: Started full-time MA at Medi-Weightloss Ballantyne (Aug 2025) — first paid clinical role, enabled by OPT
  • • What changed: 1,000+ hours direct patient care. Taking vitals, intake, working alongside PAs/NPs daily. Learned what it means to show up for patients every day
  • • Deeper lesson: Working in weight management (not dream specialty) taught that every patient deserves same quality care regardless of setting — connects to cultural humility
  • • Why UNC: Returning to the institution where she grew, researched, and studied medicine's social dimensions — now with the clinical foundation she was missing
